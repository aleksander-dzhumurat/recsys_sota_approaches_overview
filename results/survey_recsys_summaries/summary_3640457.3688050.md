### Main Idea
- The paper addresses the repeat-explore trade-off in recipe recommendations for online grocery shopping at Picnic, an online supermarket. The core question is whether recommender systems should reinforce habitual behaviors, facilitate the discovery of new items, or strike a balance between the two.
- The motivation stems from the unique characteristics of online grocery shopping, where users frequently repurchase the same items, leading to a decline in new item purchases over time. Recipes are used at Picnic to assist users in choosing meals.
- The paper presents a case study of implementing and evaluating a traditional collaborative filtering recommender and a hybrid recommender against a manually curated recipe list (baseline) to personalize the weekly menu in the Picnic app. The goal is to understand how these approaches perform in terms of balancing repeat and explore purchases.

### Technologies
- **Collaborative Filtering:** An item-item nearest neighbor recommender based on cosine similarity was used. Data augmentation techniques were employed, utilizing text embeddings from recipe instructions to identify similar recipes, addressing sparsity and cold-start issues.
- **Hybrid Recommender:** A deep neural network with a two-tower architecture was implemented to integrate content characteristics (ingredients, dietary preferences) into the recommendation process. This approach aims to improve generalization and overcome cold-start issues, supporting new and tail recipes.
- **Experimental Setup:** The study targeted 60,000 customers who frequently used the weekly menu, divided randomly into equal groups. One group served as the baseline, receiving manually curated menus. Another was exposed to collaborative filtering first, then both models were used simultaneously for a period of time with a third group exposed to the hybrid model. The evaluation period spanned 19 weeks (January to May 2024).
- **Evaluation Metrics:** Clickthrough rate (CTR) and conversion rate (number of customers who bought divided by the number who viewed) were used to evaluate performance. Chi-Squared significance tests were used to compare personalized groups to the baseline. The distribution of recommendations across repeat and explore recipes was also tracked.

### Results
- Both the collaborative filtering and hybrid recommenders significantly outperformed the manual baseline in both CTR and conversion rate.
- The collaborative filtering recommender showed an average improvement of 4% in CTR and 6.7% in conversion over the manual baseline during the 19-week period.
- The hybrid recommender showed an average improvement of 1.6% in CTR and 3% in conversion over the manual baseline during the time it ran in the study.
- The collaborative filtering model favored previously bought recipes, with an average of 55% of recommendations in the repeat category.
- The hybrid approach mainly recommended new recipes, with only 20% of recommendations falling in the repeat category.
- The collaborative filtering recommender led to 75% of conversions from previously bought recipes.
- The hybrid method resulted in 68% of converted recipe recommendations falling into the explore category.
- The paper concludes that while collaborative filtering performs best overall, the hybrid method successfully promotes new recipe discovery, potentially enhancing long-term customer satisfaction by preventing filter bubble effects. The hybrid recommender's performance is expected to improve over time.
