### Main Idea
- The paper presents a perspective on controlling quantum materials at will, focusing on manipulating exotic collective and topological phenomena.
- The motivation lies in unlocking functionalities of unprecedented capabilities in these materials to enable novel quantum technologies.
- The proposed approach involves merging state-of-the-art developments in high fluence lasers and innovative quantum material synthesis, particularly in twisted two-dimensional solids, and cavity quantum electrodynamics (QED), quantum reservoir engineering.

### Technologies
- **High Fluence Lasers**: Used for controlling materials out of equilibrium.
- **Twisted van der Waals Materials**: Employed as platforms for quantum materials engineering by controlling stacking arrangements, twist angles, or lattice constant mismatches to create moiré patterns.
- **Free Electron Lasers**: Used for generating high-energy photonic radiation to probe and excite solids beyond the standard paradigm of optics.
- **Ultrafast Time-Resolved Spectroscopic Tools**: Used for probing the changes to the materials' properties.
- **Quantum Electrodynamical Density Functional Theory (QEDFT)**: Used to explore, predict, and control light-matter hybrid states, particularly in cavity environments.
- **Non-linear Phononics**: Used to change material properties in an ultrafast, dynamic way by exciting non-linearly coupled phonon modes.
- **Floquet Engineering**: Used to gear many-body systems by a periodic drive, altering the electronic Hamiltonian effectively using the periodic perturbation by the light field.
- **Cavity-QED**: Used for cavity quantum materials engineering, where material properties are altered by the presence of cavities confining the light field and in turn enhancing the light-matter coupling geometrically.
- **Optimal Control Theory**: Used to put the system into a (close approximant of a) Floquet eigenstate, which does not evolve under drive.

### Results
- Novel control paradigms can be developed by exciting materials with external stimuli (e.g., light, pressure, strain, electric fields).
- Moiré patterns in twisted van der Waals materials allow tuning of kinetic energy scales, controlling correlated orders and topology.
- Light can induce superconductivity-like properties in certain materials at temperatures far above their equilibrium critical temperatures.
- Anomalous Hall effects can be induced in materials like graphene using circularly polarized light.
- Cavity quantum materials engineering offers potential for realizing novel ground states of coupled cavity-matter systems.
- Strong light-matter coupling leads to strongly correlated electron-photon eigenstates with intriguing topological applications.
- A combination of Cavity QED, quantum reservoir engineering and 2D-twisted van der Waals heterostructures may enable the seamless realization and control of a plethora of interacting quantum phenomena.
