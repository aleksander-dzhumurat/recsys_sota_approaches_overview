### Main Idea
- **Primary Research Objective:** The paper presents a search for eV-scale sterile neutrino oscillations using the MicroBooNE liquid argon detector. The analysis considers all possible appearance and disappearance effects within the 3+1 active-to-sterile neutrino oscillation framework. This framework posits the existence of a fourth, sterile neutrino that could explain some experimental anomalies not consistent with the standard three-flavor neutrino oscillation model.
- **Motivation and Significance:** Several experimental anomalies, such as the gallium anomaly, the reactor anomaly, LSND excess, and MiniBooNE low-energy excess, could potentially be explained by the existence of sterile neutrinos. However, these anomalies are in tension with other experimental results. MicroBooNE, with its ability to provide good electron/photon separation, offers a chance to independently test the sterile neutrino hypothesis and provide valuable insights, especially concerning the MiniBooNE low-energy excess.
- **Proposed Approach/Solution:** The study uses data from MicroBooNE's inclusive νe charged-current (CC) measurement. They compare the results to the parameter space allowed by LSND, gallium, and Neutrino-4 anomalies, simultaneously considering νe appearance and νe disappearance, and νµ disappearance. The paper also addresses a cancellation effect between νe appearance and disappearance that can lead to a degeneracy in determining oscillation parameters.

### Technologies
- **Detector:** The MicroBooNE detector is a 10.4 m long, 2.6 m wide, and 2.3 m tall liquid argon time projection chamber (LArTPC) located on-axis of the Booster Neutrino Beam (BNB) at Fermilab. It contains about 85 metric tons of liquid argon in the TPC active volume for ionization charge detection, along with an array of photomultiplier tubes for scintillation light detection.
- **Data Set:** The analysis uses the BNB Runs 1-3 data set with an exposure of 6.369 × 10^20 protons on target (POT).
- **Neutrino Beam:** The Fermilab BNB, which uses 8 GeV protons impinging on the target, producing secondary hadrons that decay in flight, resulting in a neutrino beam. The MicroBooNE BNB dataset consists of a very pure νµ beam with a small ¯νµ contamination and a νe contamination of < 1%. MicroBooNE also detects neutrinos from the NuMI beamline.
- **Analysis:** The researchers perform a full 3+1 neutrino oscillation analysis, capitalizing on the seven channels of νe and νµ selections and their statistical and systematic uncertainties from the MicroBooNE inclusive νe low-energy excess search. This includes fully contained (FC) and partially contained (PC) νe CC processes, FC and PC νµ CC processes with/without final-state π0 mesons, and a NC channel with final-state π0 mesons. A dedicated νµ → νe oscillation sample was generated to account for systematic uncertainties related to νe appearance events.
- **Neutrino Energy Reconstruction:** Primarily uses a calorimetric method with an energy resolution of ~10-15% and a bias of 5-10% for CC events.
- **PMNS Matrix:** An extended 4x4 unitary PMNS matrix (U) is used to describe the 3+1 neutrino mixing between flavor and mass eigenstates, parameterized by mixing angles (θ) and mass-squared differences (∆m^2).
- **Statistical Methods:** The test statistic used in the oscillation fit is the Combined-Neyman-Pearson (CNP) χ2. Exclusion limits are calculated using the frequentist-motivated CLs method. The Feldman-Cousins (F-C) procedure is used for p-value calculations.
- **Systematic Uncertainties:** Estimated from neutrino flux prediction of the BNB, ν-argon cross-section modeling from the GENIE event generator, final-state hadron-argon interactions in the Geant4 simulation, residual discrepancies in detector response after calibrations, and finite statistics of the MC samples.

### Results
- **Main Experimental Findings:** The MicroBooNE data shows no evidence of sterile neutrino oscillations and are consistent with the 3ν hypothesis within 1σ significance.
- **Quantitative Results:** The joint fit to the seven channels yields a best-fit result of ∆m^2_41 = 1.295 eV^2, sin^2 θ_14 = 0.936, and sin^2 θ_24 = 0 with a χ2 of 86.62 for 179 degrees of freedom. The data gives a ∆χ^2 = 2.53 with 3 degrees of freedom, corresponding to a p-value of 0.426 following the Feldman-Cousins (F-C) procedure
- **Comparisons to Anomalies:** Part of the LSND-allowed region is excluded by the MicroBooNE 2D profiled result, especially in the high and low ∆m^2_41 regions. Portions of the allowed regions of the Neutrino-4 and gallium anomalies are within the MicroBooNE data exclusion limit, with part of the region between ∆m^2_41 = 3eV^2 and 10eV^2 excluded.
- **Degeneracy Discussion:** The paper highlights a degeneracy caused by cancellation of νe appearance and disappearance effects. When sin^2 θ_24 approaches the inverse of the average value of R(Eν) in the BNB (1/R ≈ 0.005), the νe appearance and disappearance contributions mostly cancel, leading to a diminished oscillation effect in the νe channels, independent of the values of ∆m^2_41 and sin^2 θ_14.
- **Limitations and Future Work:** The MicroBooNE results are predominantly limited by the impact of the degeneracy caused by νe appearance and νe disappearance effects. Future analyses will combine the BNB and NuMI data to break the parameter degeneracy and improve sensitivity, and benefit from multi-detector strategies, as adopted by the short-baseline neutrino program (SBN).
