### Main Idea
- The paper addresses the issue of reproducibility in recommender systems research, focusing on the extent to which researchers share artifacts (code, data, documentation) needed to reproduce experimental results.
- The motivation is that a lack of reproducibility hinders scientific progress, as claims of improvements over existing methods cannot be validated if the experiments are not fully reproducible. The study aims to evaluate the current state of reproducibility in recommender systems.
- The paper's approach involves a systematic analysis of papers published at the ACM SIGIR conference (2022-2024), assessing the availability of code, data, and experimental details.

### Technologies
- **Methodology**: The study adopts a research design similar to previous work in AI reproducibility, focusing on artifact sharing rather than attempting to fully reproduce experimental results.
- **Data**: A sample of 65 papers from ACM SIGIR (2022-2024) was manually screened for relevant studies proposing new top-n collaborative filtering or sequential recommendation models.
- **Variables**: A customized set of variables was defined to assess reproducibility, grouped into the factors: Source Code, Dataset, Evaluation, and Miscellaneous.
- **Evaluation**: The paper relies on manual inspection of the papers and their repositories to categorize them based on the defined variables. The initial categorization was done by one author and cross-checked by another.

### Results
- About 75% of the inspected papers provided a link to a public repository containing code.
- While all inspected repositories contained the code for the proposed model, only about 25% shared code for at least some of the baselines.
- Hyperparameter tuning code was available in only three papers, while none provided code for statistical significance tests.
- All papers used at least one public dataset, but only about half provided working URLs for downloading them.
- Preprocessed datasets were shared in only 9% of the cases, while data splits were provided more frequently (about 54%).
- Hyperparameter ranges for the proposed models were documented in 70% of the cases, but only 16% for the baseline models. Optimal hyperparameter values were rarely reported for either the proposed or baseline models.
- Fixed embedding sizes for baseline models were used in approximately 70% of the papers, potentially leading to unfair comparisons.
- Statistical significance tests were not discussed at all in almost half of the papers.
- Only about 21% of the papers mentioned the use of an evaluation framework.
- The authors conclude that the research community has not yet reached the required level of reproducibility. They call for more rigorous reproducibility standards, including better incentives for researchers to invest in reproducibility packages and a shift in the research and publication culture to prioritize reproducibility.
