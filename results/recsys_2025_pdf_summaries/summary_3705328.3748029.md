### Main Idea
- **Problem**: The paper addresses the lack of a principled method for determining the number of quantum executions (shots) needed to achieve high-quality solutions in quantum-assisted feature selection for recommender systems, particularly in the context of noisy quantum hardware. Existing studies focus on the effectiveness and efficiency of quantum approaches but often overlook the probabilistic nature of quantum measurements.
- **Motivation and Significance**:  Recent advancements in quantum computing, especially gate-based quantum computers, show promise in outperforming traditional computing systems.  However, obtaining reliable results requires understanding how many quantum measurements (shots) are needed to compensate for the inherent noise and probabilistic nature of quantum hardware. Efficient resource allocation is crucial to leveraging quantum computers effectively.
- **Proposed Approach**: The paper proposes "Quantum-EVT" (Q-EVT), a framework based on Extreme Value Theory (EVT), to estimate the number of quantum executions (shots) required for gate-based quantum computers to achieve performance comparable to or better than classical baselines in feature selection for recommendation tasks. Q-EVT uses QAOA to solve a QUBO formulation of the feature selection problem and then applies EVT to the resulting energy values. The method also includes statistical techniques to estimate the minimum number of extreme samples needed for stable EVT parameter estimation and a method to adapt the discrete quantum outputs to be compatible with EVT by adding uniform noise.

### Technologies
- **Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA)**: Used to solve the QUBO problem on gate-based quantum devices. QAOA is a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm that leverages parameterized variational circuits.
- **Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO)**: Used to formulate the feature selection problem for recommender systems.
- **Extreme Value Theory (EVT)**: Applied to model the distribution of low-energy outcomes from repeated quantum executions, allowing estimation of the required number of repetitions to reach a target solution quality.
- **Simulated Annealing (SA)**: Used as a classical baseline for performance comparison.
- **DeepFM**: Used as the base recommendation model for generating QUBO matrices, following the PDQUBO framework.
- **AWS Braket**: Used as the quantum computing platform, including access to quantum devices like Ankaa-3 (superconducting) and Forte Enterprise 1 (ion-trap), and a simulator.
- **COBYLA**: Used for optimizing QAOA parameters.
- **Datasets**: Avazu and Criteo datasets used for click-through rate (CTR) prediction.
- **Evaluation Metrics**: Performance measured by the energy values resulting from QUBO optimization, and comparison against classical Simulated Annealing (SA). Confidence levels (95% and 90%) are used to estimate the number of runs required.
- **Statistical Tests**: Hotelling's T-squared test and multivariate Shapiro-Wilk test used for validating the stability of GEV parameter estimation.

### Results
- **Shot Estimation**: The Q-EVT framework effectively estimates the number of quantum executions (shots) required for quantum solvers to match or surpass the performance of classical simulated annealing (SA).
- **Scalability**:  The number of required quantum executions increases with the feature selection scale (i.e., the number of features considered).
- **Impact of Shots per Run**: Increasing the number of shots per run reduces the total number of runs needed to achieve the target performance.
- **Performance on Real Hardware**: Real quantum devices (Ankaa-3 and Forte) require more runs than simulators due to the presence of noise.
- **Validation**:  Empirical evaluations on quantum simulators showed that the EVT-based predictions accurately pinpoint the required number of repetitions, with the empirical curves intersecting close to the predicted point.
- **Hardware Evaluation**:  Experiments on the Ankaa-3 QPU demonstrated that increasing the number of shots per run improved the probability of finding high-quality solutions, with performance converging towards the SA baseline as the shot budget increased.
- **Limitations**: The EVT-based framework may break down when the quantum algorithm consistently yields identical optimal outputs within a small number of shots or when the quantum outputs remain significantly suboptimal even after many executions.
