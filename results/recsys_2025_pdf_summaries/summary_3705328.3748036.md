### Main Idea
- The paper addresses the problem of inefficient optimization in self-supervised graph learning for recommender systems. Existing methods use a multi-task learning framework with separate supervised recommendation loss and self-supervised contrastive loss, which leads to redundant graph convolutions, inconsistent gradient directions, and prolonged training times.
- The paper's motivation is to improve the efficiency and performance of graph-based recommender systems by addressing the limitations of existing self-supervised learning approaches.
- The proposed solution is Supervised Graph Contrastive Learning (SGCL), a unified framework that combines the training of recommendation and unsupervised contrastive losses into a single supervised contrastive learning loss. This aligns both tasks within a single optimization direction for faster training and better performance.

### Technologies
- **Supervised Graph Contrastive Learning (SGCL) Loss:** A novel loss function that integrates user-item interaction supervision directly within the self-supervised loss.
- **Light Graph Convolution:** A graph convolution operation where embeddings of user and item are updated by consistently aggregating the connected neighbours' representations.
- **User-Item Bipartite Graph:** Represents user-item interactions as a graph structure.
- **Datasets:** Amazon Beauty and Toys-and-Games datasets were used for evaluation.
- **Evaluation Metrics:** Recall@20, Recall@50, NDCG@20, and NDCG@50 were used to measure recommendation performance.
- **Baselines:** The method was compared to BiasMF, NeuMF, NGCF, LightGCN, NCL, SGL, LightGCL, and SimGCL.

### Results
- SGCL outperformed state-of-the-art methods on the Beauty and Toys-and-Games datasets, achieving higher NDCG and Recall scores.
- SGCL demonstrated faster convergence speed and lower total training time compared to other graph contrastive recommendation baselines like LightGCN and SGL.
- The results highlight the benefits of the proposed supervised graph contrastive learning approach in terms of both accuracy and efficiency, demonstrating the effectiveness of integrating supervised signals directly into the self-supervised learning process.
