### Main Idea

- The paper addresses the lack of comprehensive datasets that combine user-recipe interactions with reliable environmental impact data for food recommendation systems. It aims to facilitate the development of recommendation tools that consider both user preferences and sustainability, specifically carbon footprint (CFP) and water footprint (WFP).
- The motivation stems from the increasing consumer awareness of sustainability and the significant environmental impact of the food supply chain. Existing food recommender systems primarily focus on user engagement and recipe ingredients, neglecting the environmental aspect.
- The proposed solution is GreenFoodLens, a dataset that enriches HUMMUS, a large corpus for food recommendation, with environmental impact estimates derived from the SU-EATABLE-LIFE project's hierarchical taxonomy. This involves a multi-step process of taxonomy revision, human and LLM-based label assignment, and constrained generation to ensure accurate and reliable environmental labels for ingredients in recipes.

### Technologies

- **Dataset**: HUMMUS (user-recipe interaction network) enriched with GreenFoodLens labels.
- **Environmental Data**: SU-EATABLE-LIFE taxonomy providing CFP and WFP values for food commodities in a hierarchical structure.
- **Taxonomy Revision**: Modification of the SU-EATABLE-LIFE taxonomy to improve interpretability and enable more precise mappings (splitting ANIMAL HUSBANDRY and adding intermediate levels).
- **Human Annotation**: Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk) was used to collect human annotations for ingredient labeling.
- **Large Language Models (LLMs)**:
    - NexusFlow Athene-V2-Chat with 72B parameters (Athene)
    - Nvidia Llama-3.1-Nemotron-Instruct with 70B parameters (Llama-3.1)
- **Constrained Generation (CG)**: Grammar-based CG using GGML Backus-Naur Form (GBNF) to ensure LLM outputs adhere strictly to the taxonomy and avoid hallucinations.
- **Prompting Techniques**: Few-shot prompting and generate-knowledge prompting to improve the accuracy of LLM labeling.
- **Recommendation Models for Evaluation**:
    - Pop/MostPop (traditional)
    - BPR (Bayesian Personalized Ranking, matrix factorization)
    - LightGCN (graph neural network)
    - DiffRec (diffusion model)
    - MultiVAE (variational autoencoder)
    - KGAT (knowledge-aware model)
- **Evaluation Metrics**:
    - Utility metrics: NDCG and Recall (Rec)
    - Beyond-accuracy metrics: Item Coverage (ICov), Gini Index (Gini), Tail Ratio (Tail), and Novelty (Nov)
- **Implementation**: Recbole library for recommender systems.

### Results

- LLM labeling accuracy: Athene consistently outperformed Llama-3.1 across all taxonomy levels, with the largest gaps at the sub-typology and item levels. Accuracy declines steadily from Group Match (GM) to Item Match (IM), yet both models correctly assign food groups and typologies for at least 68% of ingredients.
- GreenFoodLens provides comprehensive coverage of the SU-EATABLE-LIFE hierarchy (558 items across four levels) and assigns CFP and WFP values to every ingredient in all 507,335 HUMMUS recipes, outperforming existing datasets with incomplete coverage or coarse category mappings.
- Experiments using recommendation models on HUMMUS with GreenFoodLens labels show that models are influenced by popularity signals, which may exacerbate the environmental impact of users' recipe choices.
- The distribution of CFP and WFP in recommended recipes generally follows user interactions, but models' behavior is largely shaped by data rather than explicit sustainability awareness.
- Limitations include reliance on the prior LLM knowledge and potential ambiguity in labeling ingredients without additional context. Future work will explore incorporating external knowledge, refining the labeling process, and addressing more environmental perspectives with additional metrics.
