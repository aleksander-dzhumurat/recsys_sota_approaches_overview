### Main Idea
- The paper addresses the largely unexplored issue of product-side fairness in bundle recommendation (BR). BR involves recommending sets of items (bundles), which adds complexity because user satisfaction and product/supplier exposure depend on both the bundle and its constituent items.
- The motivation is that existing fairness frameworks for traditional recommendation systems might not be directly applicable to BR's multi-layered structure. The significance lies in ensuring fair exposure for products/suppliers in the emerging BR domain, which is increasingly common in various applications.
- The paper conducts a comprehensive reproducibility study of product-side fairness in BR using real-world datasets and state-of-the-art BR methods, analyzing exposure disparities at both bundle and item levels and considering the impact of user interaction preferences.

### Technologies
- **Datasets:** Youshu (books), NetEase (music playlists), and iFashion (fashion outfits).
- **BR Methods:** CrossCBR, MultiCBR, EBRec, and BunCa. These methods represent multi-view learning, graph neural networks, and data augmentation approaches.
- **Fairness Metrics:** Six widely adopted exposure fairness metrics, categorized under equal opportunity (Exposed Utility Ratio (EUR), Realized Utility Ratio (RUR), Expected Exposure Loss (EEL), Expected Exposure Relevance (EER)) and statistical parity (Expected Exposure Disparity (EED), Demographic Parity (DP)).
- **Evaluation Setup:** Datasets are split into training, validation, and test sets. Utility is measured using Recall@K and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain@K. Geometric browsing model used to compute exposure. User grouping strategy to investigate the impact of user preferences.
- **Novelty:** A novel approach is used to investigate the impact of user preferences on bundle- and item-level fairness through a user grouping strategy based on the tendency to interact more with bundles versus individual items.

### Results
- Exposure patterns differ notably between bundles and items, highlighting the need for fairness interventions beyond bundle-level assumptions.
- Fairness assessments vary considerably depending on the metric used, reinforcing the need for multi-faceted evaluation.
- User behavior plays a critical role: when users interact more frequently with bundles than individual items, BR systems tend to yield fairer exposure distributions across both levels.
- BR methods typically decrease the uniformity of exposure compared to the input data, indicating an amplification of popularity bias. Datasets with less uniform interaction distributions tend to result in less uniform exposure distributions.
- Achieving fairness at the bundle level does not necessarily translate to fairness at the item level.
- MultiCBR and BunCa consistently perform well in terms of accuracy, while CrossCBR and EBRec tend to outperform others in bundle-level fairness.
